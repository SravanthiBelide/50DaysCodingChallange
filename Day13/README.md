## &#x20;Stress Level Analysis:



###### Dataset:



EmployeeID Unique employee identifier 

Department Employee department 

Age Employee age 

Gender Male/Female 

WorkHours Average daily work hours 

SleepHours Average daily sleep hours 

ExerciseHours Average weekly exercise hours 

CaffeineIntake Daily coffee/tea cups 

StressLevel Stress rating 



StressLevel: Rating from 1 (Low) to 5 (Very High).

Work/Life Factors: Work hours per day, sleep hours per day, and weekly exercise hours.

Personal Habits: Daily caffeine intake (cups of coffee/tea).

Demographics: Age, Gender, and Department.



###### KPI Tracking: 

Company-wide Average Stress Level.

Percentage of employees with "High Stress" 

Ratio of Average Sleep Hours vs. Average Work Hours.

###### Visualizations:

Stress trends over time and departmental stress comparisons.

Correlation analysis using scatter plots (e.g., Work Hours vs. Stress).

Distribution charts for Low, Medium, and High stress categories

.Advanced Features:DAX Measures: 

Custom formulas for High Stress %, Sleep-Work ratio, and Avg Stress.Conditional Formatting: Specifically highlighting "at-risk" employees (Stress Level 5 AND Sleep < 5 hours).





###### Insights report:



\- IT Department shows consistently high stress levels — long working hours (often 10–11 hours) combined with high caffeine intake are strong contributors.

\- Operations employees display wide variation — some with very high stress due to extended shifts and low sleep, while others manage better with balanced routines.

\- HR Department has mixed outcomes — several employees report low stress when sleep hours are adequate, but others show spikes linked to poor rest and high caffeine.

\- Marketing staff often face elevated stress — especially those with shorter sleep durations and higher workloads; younger employees in this department also show medium stress despite lower caffeine intake.

\- Finance Department trends toward higher stress — many employees work long hours with limited sleep, and caffeine intake is a frequent factor in stress escalation.
